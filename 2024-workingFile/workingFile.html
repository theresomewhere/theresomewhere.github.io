Case Study #4: Search Experiences

Projects

During much of the past 2 years, I worked with a large tech industry leader on a of variety Search and search-related rapid research studies.

I conducted over 40 studies during this time that involved foundational and directional research methodologies to drive product decisions. Methodologies included in-depth interviews, user-testing, journey-mapping, and literature reviews.

Studies varied from understanding user mental models of LLM chatbots, the impact of generative AI augmented search results on trust, how virtual assistants impacted search journeys, and how search results impacted users seeking information about elections.

Project Scope

Client: tech industry leader

Goal: provide UX feedback from first external participants pre & post launch of LLM AI chatbot

Audience: UX design team working with engineering at launch

Scope: iterative weekly directional studies

"Show us how real people break it, so we can fix it before launch."

Methodology

In-person in-depth interviews: ~40 participants with varying experience levels with LLM chatbots. Participants included tech-savvy early adopters while others had limited to no experience with chatbots

Iterative focus: learn how people interact with LLM chatbots, perform a competitive analysis, map user journeys, and discover mental models

Key Findings

Assumption: users expect more than search from an AI chat tool

Reality: participants expected more than search, however, were not prepared to effectively interact in an open-ended conversation with the chatbot

Assumption: users would interact conversationally with the chatbot

Reality: open-ended conversations revealed limitations for purpose-driven tasks as participants were not prepared to drive the conversation and expressed the desire for more guidance from the chatbot

Assumption: interacting with a chat tool requires different interactions than a search omni-box

Reality: expectations of keyboard shortcut interactions were undermined by confusion from participants expecting to point and click

Assumption: users had realistic use-cases for an AI chatbot

Reality: Some participants demonstrated writing assistance tasks and/or searching for recipes. Other participants expected the chatbot to solve problems and were surprised to learn of the chatbot's limitations

Favorite Research Moment

I'll never forget the excitement and joy participants with no AI chatbot experience shared after receiving a particularly effective response to a prompt.

Experiencing how skeptical participants became animated at the possibilities of a tool that was about to roll out the gate was wonderful feeback to be able to share with the teams working so hard to bring to launch.

 

"When will I be able to use this at home?!?"

 

Analysis process

Analysis varied throughout the iterative process and included a variety of qualitative methods. A rapid research protocol was employed in order to plan for sessions, learn from participants, report, iterate, all while planning for the next week's study.

Project Outcomes

The research helped the stakeholder:

  • Implement design decisions
  • Guide future research
  • Better understand how user mental models changed over time as they gained experience of AI chatbots

"Amazing work, you wrangled it all together and got some quick insights for our team to learn from."

Sample Study: Vehicle Configurator

Project Scope

Client: automotive industry leader

Goal: Convert users completing the configuration process into users taking the next step to engage with the dealership

Audience:

  • Outdoor enthusiasts actively pursuing the purchase of an off-road capable sport utility vehicle. For example:
    • off-roading
    • mountain biking
    • climbing
    • hiking/backpacking
  • Finds value in vehicle customization

Research questions:

  • What barriers/roadblocks do users encounter in the vehicle configuration process?
  • What configurator terminology is clear/unclear to users?
  • Do users know the next steps after following the configuration process?

"QUOTE HERE"

Methodology

In-depth interviews: Participants were tasked with selecting a vehicle configuration with task success/fail rate observed.Moderator probed success/fail moments.

Key Findings

Assumption: Multiple configurator interaction models contribute to user interaction failures

Reality: Participants confidence level was undermined as interaction modes differed when selecting options.

Assumption: The configurator terminology would be familiar to the intended audience.

Reality: Participants found the configurator terminology to be clear and the steps of the configurator were easy to act on
