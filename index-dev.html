Ph.D. Research

Toward the Development of Usability Guidelines for Single-window Web Interfaces

Since the early 1990s, usability research has guided development of web interfaces used to interact with content available on the Internet. Following these guidelines has resulted in web pages that in many characteristics are quite similar and are identified here as Conventional Web Interfaces (CWIs). An emergent genre of web interface, the Single Window Interface (SWI), differs in many ways from CWIs. Most importantly, SWIs differ from CWIs in the type of tasks expected of their users and in the visual strategies used to facilitate these tasks. Namely, SWIs facilitate open-ended discovery tasks by using strong visual cues to convey meta-information to the user and encourage both the exploration and perusal of content.

Ph.D. Research

“Toward the Development of Usability Guidelines for Single-window Web Interfaces” demonstrated that the differences between SWIs and CWIs require revisiting current usability guidelines in order to determine how to guide future development of SWIs. If SWI visual strategies can be shown to be effective in conveying meta-information qualities to users, the groundwork will be prepared for future research investigating the effectiveness of these strategies in facilitating open-ended exploration and discovery within SWIs. These efforts will lead to more useful experiences for users of SWIs and inform the fields of technical communication as well as human-computer interaction and usability research, to name just a few of the affected fields of study.

The 20-year history of usability research encourages the use of similar characteristics to create web pages with the goal of completing tasks effectively and efficiently. Interestingly, these web pages are seemingly more related to the static paper documents that preceded them, rather than dynamic documents taking full advantage of the responsive qualities screen-based media can convey. Furthermore, this usability research discourages the use of strong visual cues (graphic elements and/or animation) to convey meta-information (information about information) qualities to a user of web interfaces. Meta-information qualities may include relatedness, proximity, or paths and can show relationships between content available to a user. Although discouraged by current usability research, the use of strong visual cues will be familiar to [per]users of SWIs.

Single-window Web Interfacess present available content without (necessarily) a specific goal in mind for the user, leading to a new type of user, the peruser.

SWIs are significant in two ways. First, SWIs encourage open-ended exploration and discovery of content by providing meta-information about the available content using pronounced visual strategies. Second, SWIs present available content without (necessarily) a specific goal in mind for the user, leading to a new type of user, the peruser. The peruser consumes web content in a much different way than the current concept of a user of web content. A user has a discrete task at hand including an intended result, whereas a peruser consumes web content intending to explore and discover, rather than completing discrete tasks. The combination of these issues points to the emergence of a new genre of web interface. This emergent genre appears to be attempting to provide perusers a way to explore the ever-expanding quantity of information gathered as web content. This emergence also warrants revisiting usability guidelines in an effort to develop web interfaces that can reduce the “noise” that accompanies the ever-expanding quantity of web content. Schonfeld (2008) identifies the noise of web content when describing his efforts to manage his ever-growing social media streams from Twitter and Friendfeed. Schonfeld installed Twhirl to solve one problem, his inability to keep up with his online social contacts using the web interfaces provided by those social services, and identified another bigger, more difficult problem to solve. Namely, “[t]here is too much to pay attention to and not enough ways to reduce the noise” (see below).

What visual cues convey relatedness within a searchable field of items such as the interfaces found within SWIs?

Will single or multiple visual cues be more effective in conveying relatedness within a searchable field of items such as the interfaces found within SWIs?

Experimental Study

  • Recruit users from multiple audiences
  • Account for lifestyle computing rather than only business computing
  • Use of visual strategies to convey meta-information
  • Further development of usability guidelines
  • Participants exposed to two independent variables
    • Number of visual cues Single and multiple cues
    • Type of visual cue Color, Proximity, Path
  • Participants tracked on two dependent variables
    • Time
    • Accuracy

Participants

  • Over 233,000 points of data collected
  • 161 total participants
  • 139 participants completed experiment & follow-up questionnaire
  • 58% (81) female, 42% (58) male

Analysis

  • Multiple visual cues (p = 0.039) showed significant effects in conveying relatedness faster rather than single visual cues (p = 0.116)
  • Multiple visual cues (p = 0.022) showed significant effects in conveying relatedness with fewer errors rather than single visual cues (p = 0.158)

Findings

  • It is interesting to note that when given the opportunity to select between visual cue combinations, participants always selected a single cue or multiple cues that included the visual cue path.
  • When given the opportunity to select between a single cue or multiple cues, more participants selected the combination of cues with more visual cues included in the combination of cues.
  • Therefore, it can be said with some confidence that multiple cues are better at conveying relatedness than single cues.
  • Participants interacted efficiently with the experimental interface.
  • Mean response time to identify the related items of 1.33 seconds for single visual cues and 1.20 seconds for multiple visual cues.
  • “I didn’t understand what I was supposed to do at first, but after a couple rounds it made sense and then it was easy.”
  • Participants interacted accurately with the experimental interface.
  • Mean number of clicks to identify the related items was 2.19 for single visual cues and 2.03 for multiple visual cues.
  • “It was pretty clear as to what objects were most related.”
  • Usability research should recognize and acknowledge that an evolving purpose for more and more users of web content is revealing itself:
  • The perusal of content rather than using content
  • Perusal of content is significantly changing the definitions of what a successful interaction entails.
  • The visual strategies of SWIs can inform the direction of future web content interface development.

After my grandparents passed, my mother extended the invitation to come to the house and retrieve items to remember grandma (Elizabeth [above left]) and grandpa (Frank [above right]). I left with a victorian chair lowered from the attic window, some black and white photographs taken with my trusty Pentax K1000, a Jaguar two-seater tootsie toy, a larger Greyhound Bus 32-seater tootsie toy, and a number of 3″ x 5″ black and white family photo negatives. After printing all of the number of 3″ x 5″ black and white family photo negatives in my bathroom darkroom, I took the developed photos to my classroom critique where the professor questioned the permissions I had to create with these photos. Yes, my mother and her sister, Sister Mary. So yes, Mother and Sister gave me permission. The following gallery shares the depiction. Seven books sewn with a thread taller than myself during a night longer than many others in Champaign in a room that was narrower to the north, wider to the south, and where the western light gave flight to many a fancy.
