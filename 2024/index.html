Moments of Time

After my grandparents passed, my mother extended the invitation to come to the house and retrieve items to remember grandma (Elizabeth [above left]) and grandpa (Frank [above right]). I left with a victorian chair lowered from the attic window, some black and white photographs taken with my trusty Pentax K1000, a Jaguar two-seater tootsie toy, a larger Greyhound Bus 32-seater tootsie toy, and a number of 3 x 5 black and white family photo negatives. After printing all of the number of 3 x 5 black and white family photo negatives in my bathroom darkroom, I took the developed photos to my classroom critique where the professor questioned the permissions I had to create with these photos. Yes, my mother and her sister, Sister Mary. So yes, Mother and Sister gave me permission. The following gallery shares the depiction. Seven books sewn with a thread taller than myself during a night longer than many others in Champaign in a room that was narrower to the north, wider to the south, and where the western light gave flight to many a fancy.

One more thing

Another The Everydays Series response to the question "Does trust have a meaning anymore?".

Threshold of trust



I commute from Evanston to 31st and State Street in Chicago and back most every working day. Especially in winter, there must be trust between more than a few things. Me. The bike. The weather. The cars. The buildings that block the wind. If a building that once was there is no longer there the wind finds a way through.

The building, the restaurant that once was here was a place. Waiters, waitresses, cooks, food, hostesses, proprietors, diners, meals, tables, booths, and on some nights, musicians their families and fans. You trust that things will always be. But things change. They always do. My daughter played music here. Played guitar with her class. Her teacher tuned her guitar.

Her class performed with her teachers.

Her brother looked on and on.

The wind finds its way through here but I trust the memories to keep me warm.
